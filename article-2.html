EPA Boosts Estimate Of U.S. Methane Emissions

Click on the image below for the original article.
A new report by the EPA revises upward its estimates of U.S. methane emissions by more than 3.4 million metric tons.

Summary

In a report on April 15th, the EPA revised their U.S. methane emission estimate from 25.453 million metric tons to 28.859 million metric tons. That's an increase of more than 3.4 million metric tons, or a 13% increase in the total U.S. methane emissions estimate. 2/3 of this estimate comes from natural gas and petroleum sectors, and the rest comes from landfills. In that report, the EPA also provided the first estimate of methane emissions for 2014. There's a slight increase to 29.233 million metric tons from the U.S. methane emissions estimate for 2013. Even with the increase in the EPA's U.S. methane emissions estimate, many studies have shown that the EPA is still significantly underestimating the U.S. share of global methane emissions, which accounts for about 1/4 of anthropogenic global warming. David Lyon, an environmental scientist at the Environmental Defense Fund, says that even with the higher methane estimates, the EPA is still about 20% to 60% off. The EPA's reports influence U.S. regulations of methane-producing industries like agriculture and fossil fuel production. A large part of the methane not being accounted for may come from "super emitters" that are typically malfunctioning equipment, making it hard for the EPA to account for them.
