Sea Levels Could Rise Twice As Fast As Previously Predicted

Click on the image below for the original article.
The collapse of keystone glaciers in West Antarctica could raise sea levels faster and sooner than thought.

Summary

​The melting of Antarctica's glaciers could cause sea levels to rise by about 64 to 114 centimeters by 2100, as suggested by a simulation of Antarctica's thawing ice. Antarctica's melting ice isn't the only factor involved when predicting future sea levels. The expansion of warmer seawaters and the melting of Greenland ice is also involved with rising sea levels, and because of that, scientists predict that sea levels will rise 1.5 to 2.1 meters by the end of the century. However, predicting future sea levels requires an understanding of past ocean levels, so scientists often reconstruct ancient coastlines to simulate past ocean levels. The problem is, forces like tectonic plate activity causes disruptions, so these simulations may not be accurate. Richard Alley, a Penn State glaciologist, says "If we continue warming the world rapidly, the most likely outcome is a major event of large and rapid sea level rise.” Two warm periods that occurred 125 thousand years ago and 3 million years ago helped scientists fine-tune a computer simulation of how climate change affects Antarctic ice. Using that information, researchers were able to project sea level rise thousands of years into the future. The simulation shows that the melting of Antarctic ice will increase even more in 2050, and in 2100, Antarctica will contribute over 4 centimeters of sea level rise, which is over 10 times the current rate. These high rates of rising sea levels could drastically reshape Earth's coastlines.

Reflection

This article's title, along with the picture above, caught my eye as I was looking through the ScienceNews website. I've done several projects and written a research paper on glaciers before, so I figured I could read this article and easily summarize/comment on it, because I'm fairly knowledgeable when it comes to glaciers. This article didn't come as much of a surprise to me. I already knew that sea levels were rising dangerously fast due to melting glaciers. I didn't know the exact numbers, though, so that was something I learned after reading this article. As I was reading the part about Earth's coastlines possibly being reshaped due to the rise in sea levels, I started thinking about what our coasts would look like if they were reshaped and what effect that would have. Possibly reshaping the coastline certainly is a huge deal, yet not many people seem to be doing anything about it. Not only that, in class, as we were learning about the hydrosphere, we learned that 3% of all water on Earth is freshwater and 68.7% of that freshwater is found in glaciers and ice caps. Losing glaciers and ice caps would mean losing more freshwater.
