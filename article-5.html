The Effort To Save The Northern White Rhino

Click on the image below for the original article.
Rhino caretaker Mohammed Doyo caresses 25-year-old female northern white rhino.

Summary

In December of 2015, a group of scientists from around the world met in Austria to talk about northern white rhinos and how to bring it back from the brink of extinction. They discussed topics like genetics, cell biology, ethics, and long term strategic thinking and communication to save the northern white rhinos. One of the main topics of discussion was the need to keep genetic banks of frozen tissue, sperm, and eggs of the remaining northern white rhinos to use as materials to fight their extinction. Geneticists are hoping to be able to use those materials to create stem cells and hopefully produce viable northern white rhinos. Thomas Hildebrandt, Prof. Dr., head of the Reproduction Management department at IZW, said that he's glad that there are so many supporters in the scientific community who agree that using advanced cellular/reproductive technologies can help rescue species on the brink of extinction such as the northern white rhino. There are only 3 remaining northern white rhinos left in the entire world, and they're located in Ol Pejeta Conservancy in Kenya. The health of the rhinos no longer allow them to breed naturally, so the research discussed in Vienna will give the northern white rhinos the chance to see offspring of their own kind. Not only did the scientists that met in Austria talk about reproductive strategies to help counter the extinction of the rhinos, they talked about the ethics of it all as well. The discussions of the meeting in Vienna were published in the international Journal Zoo Biology to voice the hope that the information can be used to save other species from extinction and hopefully raise awareness as well.

Reflection
